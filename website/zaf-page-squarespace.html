The problem

Most organisations produce artefacts. Few produce decisions.

Enterprise architecture frameworks are sequenced incorrectly. The standard process runs: assess the current state → produce artefacts → have conversations → make decisions. By the time the artefact is complete, the decision has already been made by default — in a hallway, in a budget meeting, or by whoever was loudest in the room.

The result is documentation that demonstrates process compliance and delivers nothing. The capability map that took twelve weeks and sits on a SharePoint nobody opens. The integration architecture that was accurate when written and is wrong now. The architecture vision that everyone endorsed and nobody follows.

The artefact problem

Artefacts are produced as evidence of rigour rather than as instruments of decision. The production process becomes the goal. The decision becomes the by-product — or does not happen at all.

The speed problem

Organisations move at the speed of their decisions. Architecture practices move at the speed of their frameworks. When these diverge, architecture arrives after the commitment is made and ratifies what was already decided.

The audience problem

EA artefacts are designed to demonstrate framework compliance to other architects. Executives need a recommendation and an ask. The gap between those two outputs is where EA loses credibility.

The accessibility problem

Conventional EA practice assumes a dedicated architecture team, a long programme, and the resources to produce comprehensive documentation. Most organisations do not have any of those things. They still have the same decisions to make.

The position

ZAF inverts the sequence.

The conversation happens first. The artefact records the commitment made in that conversation — at the resolution required to protect the decision over time. Nothing more. This is not a shortcut. It is the correct practice.

The classification

Every EA artefact resolves to one of four types.

The type determines the value trajectory, the maintenance obligation, and the failure mode. Derived from Kotusev's empirical analysis of 47 organisations — the most extensive published empirical study of EA practice.

Type 1

Decision instruments

Force commitment and shift the burden of proof. Peak value at the point of commitment. Decay as assumptions age. Architecture Vision, Application Portfolio, Technology Roadmap.

Failure mode: produced without forcing a decision

Type 2

Control mechanisms

Operate without authority through the exception process. Value accumulates as the exception record grows. Architecture Principles, Standards Catalogue, Compliance Review.

Failure mode: no exception process attached

Type 3

Signal generators

Make invisible problems undeniable. Value peaks at delivery, decays without structural response. Business Capability Map, Value Stream Map, Organisation Map.

Failure mode: acknowledged and not acted upon

Type 4

Ground truth artefacts

Accuracy-dependent organisational memory. Maintained artefacts hold value indefinitely. Unmaintained ones decay at the rate of organisational change. Integration Architecture, Infrastructure Architecture, ADRs.

Failure mode: produced once and allowed to drift
