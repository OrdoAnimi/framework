# Velocity Decision Maturity Assessment

**ZenCloud Global Consultants**  
Classification: Advisory Tool — Engagement Use  
Version: 1.0 — May 2026

---

## Purpose

The Velocity Decision Maturity Assessment (VDMA) is a structured diagnostic used at the start of enterprise architecture advisory engagements.

It gives ZenCloud a baseline of how an organisation currently makes, records, and governs architecture decisions. It gives the client a clear picture of where they are and what a decision-first operating model would change.

The VDMA is not a report card. It is a starting point for the conversation.

---

## Maturity Levels

| Level | Label | Characteristics |
|-------|-------|----------------|
| **1** | Ad hoc | Decisions happen. Nothing is recorded. |
| **2** | Document-heavy | Everything is documented. Decisions are buried in documents. |
| **3** | Governed but slow | Governance exists. It is a bottleneck, not an accelerator. |
| **4** | Decision-led | Decisions are explicit, recorded, and traceable. Governance is fast. |
| **5** | AI-assisted and traceable | Decision-led model with AI-accelerated artefact production and full traceability from business outcome to delivery. |

---

## Maturity Dimensions

### 1. Decision Clarity

*Can the organisation articulate the specific decisions that govern its major programs and platforms?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Decisions are implicit. No one can name the last five architecture decisions made. |
| 2 | Decisions are embedded in documents. You can find them if you read everything. |
| 3 | Key decisions are known but not formally recorded or linked to outcomes. |
| 4 | Decisions are named, recorded, and linked to business outcomes. |
| 5 | Decision register is live, accessible, and updated throughout delivery. |

---

### 2. Artefact Usefulness

*Do architecture artefacts actually influence decisions — or do they get filed and ignored?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Artefacts exist to satisfy governance gates. No one reads them after sign-off. |
| 2 | Artefacts are produced in volume. Quality is inconsistent. Reuse is rare. |
| 3 | Standard artefact formats exist. Usage is inconsistent across teams. |
| 4 | Artefacts are decision-derived. Every artefact can be traced to a decision question. |
| 5 | Artefacts are generated, validated, and updated using AI tooling under practitioner review. |

---

### 3. Governance Speed

*How long does it take to get an architecture decision through governance?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | No formal governance. Decisions are made informally and silently. |
| 2 | Governance exists but is opaque. Time to decision is unpredictable. |
| 3 | Governance cadence is defined. Average time to decision is 4–8 weeks. |
| 4 | Governance is lean. Decisions are pre-framed and board-ready. Average time to decision is 1–2 weeks. |
| 5 | Governance is a decision-support function, not a bottleneck. Board packs are AI-assisted and practitioner-validated. |

---

### 4. Traceability

*Can the organisation trace a current delivery decision back to its original business rationale?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | No traceability. "We've always done it this way." |
| 2 | Some decisions have documented rationale. Most do not. |
| 3 | ADR format exists. Adoption is inconsistent. |
| 4 | All significant decisions have an ADR. ADRs are linked to program records. |
| 5 | Full traceability from business outcome → architecture decision → delivery milestone. AI-assisted search and retrieval. |

---

### 5. Architecture / Delivery Alignment

*Do delivery teams know what architecture decisions govern their work?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Architecture and delivery operate in separate streams. Alignment is accidental. |
| 2 | Architecture handover happens. Delivery teams do not always follow it. |
| 3 | Architecture governance touchpoints are defined. Compliance is inconsistent. |
| 4 | Architecture decisions are communicated to delivery teams at each sprint boundary. |
| 5 | PMO Portal and decision register are shared across architecture and delivery. Drift is detected early. |

---

### 6. Business Outcome Alignment

*Are architecture decisions traceable to business outcomes — not just technical requirements?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Architecture is driven by technical preference. Business outcomes are not referenced. |
| 2 | Business requirements exist. Architecture decisions are not always linked to them. |
| 3 | Business case exists for major programs. Architecture alignment is assumed. |
| 4 | Every architecture decision is evaluated against defined business outcome criteria. |
| 5 | Business value lens is applied at every decision gate. Benefits realisation is tracked. |

---

### 7. Data Readiness

*Is the organisation's data in a state that supports the decisions it is trying to make?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Data quality is unknown. Decisions are made on gut or incomplete data. |
| 2 | Data quality issues are known but unresolved. Architecture decisions proceed anyway. |
| 3 | Data governance exists. Data quality is monitored in some domains. |
| 4 | Data readiness is assessed at each architecture decision gate. |
| 5 | Continuous data quality pipeline. AI readiness of data is assessed as part of every decision. |

---

### 8. Security Integration

*Is security a decision input — or a late-stage sign-off?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Security is a compliance check at go-live. Not involved in architecture decisions. |
| 2 | Security reviews happen. They are not integrated into architecture decision-making. |
| 3 | Security is a governance gate. Architecture must pass security review before approval. |
| 4 | Security risk lens is applied at every architecture decision point. Risks are recorded in the risk register. |
| 5 | Security decisions are traceable, quantified, and linked to business risk appetite. |

---

### 9. AI Readiness

*Is the organisation prepared to adopt AI — and are its architecture decisions AI-ready?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | AI is being discussed at SLT level. No governance framework exists. |
| 2 | AI pilots are running. No consistent evaluation criteria applied. |
| 3 | AI strategy exists. Adoption is uneven. Governance lags implementation. |
| 4 | AI readiness lens is applied at each architecture decision. Data and security prerequisites are confirmed before adoption. |
| 5 | AI is integrated into the architecture operating model — in both tool use (VAF Agentic AI) and client capability assessment. |

---

### 10. Repository Health

*Is the organisation's architecture knowledge accessible and current?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Architecture artefacts are scattered across SharePoint, email, and personal drives. |
| 2 | A repository exists. It is rarely updated. Search is poor. |
| 3 | A structured repository exists. It is used inconsistently. |
| 4 | Repository is current, searchable, and maintained as a live resource. |
| 5 | Repository is version-controlled, linked to the decision register, and AI-searchable. |

---

### 11. PMO Alignment

*Does the PMO understand and use architecture decisions in program governance?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | PMO and architecture are separate functions with no shared artefacts. |
| 2 | Architecture sign-off is a PMO gate. Content is not integrated. |
| 3 | Architecture status is reported to PMO. Format is inconsistent. |
| 4 | Architecture decision register is referenced in PMO governance reporting. |
| 5 | PMO Portal integrates architecture status, decision register, risk register, and delivery health in one view. |

---

### 12. Practitioner Capability

*Does the organisation have practitioners who can operate a decision-first architecture model?*

| Level | Characteristics |
|-------|----------------|
| 1 | Architecture is done by whoever is available. No defined capability. |
| 2 | Architecture practitioners exist. Methods are inconsistent. |
| 3 | Architecture capability is defined. Some practitioners follow a structured method. |
| 4 | Practitioners are trained in decision-first methods. Artefact standards are followed consistently. |
| 5 | Velocity-certified practitioners operate the full four-box model with AI-assisted tooling under practitioner accountability. |

## Assessment Method

### Workshop Format

A VDMA workshop is structured as a 2-hour facilitated session with the architecture and delivery leadership team.

**Participants:**
- Enterprise Architect or Architecture Lead
- Solution Architect representative
- PMO Lead or Program Director
- CTO, CIO, or delegate (optional but recommended)

**Format:**
1. Introduction to the decision-first model (15 minutes)
2. Dimension-by-dimension self-assessment (60 minutes) — facilitated discussion, not a survey
3. Scoring consolidation and pattern identification (20 minutes)
4. Output discussion and initial recommendations (25 minutes)

The workshop produces a scored baseline and a prioritised set of observations. It is not a report — it is the starting point for an engagement conversation.

---

## Scoring Guide

Each dimension is scored 1–5.

Score the dimension at the level where the organisation's characteristics predominantly fit. If the organisation is between levels, score the lower level and note the partially-met characteristics of the higher.

| Score range | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| 12–24 | Ad hoc or document-heavy. High opportunity for decision-first uplift. |
| 25–36 | Governed but slow. Structural governance exists. Decision quality and speed need attention. |
| 37–48 | Decision-led in some domains. Inconsistent across the organisation. |
| 49–60 | Decision-led and AI-ready. Velocity can accelerate and extend what is already working. |

---

## Evidence to Collect

Before or during the workshop, request:

- A sample architecture board pack or governance submission (most recent)
- A sample ADR or equivalent decision record (if they exist)
- The most recent risk register for a major program
- PMO status report for a current delivery
- Any existing architecture principles or reference architecture documents
- Organisation chart for EA/SA/PMO functions

---

## Outputs

1. **Maturity score** per dimension — visual radar chart recommended
2. **Overall level** — the modal level across dimensions
3. **Priority observations** — three to five patterns most impacting decision quality
4. **Initial recommendations** — matched to the gap between current level and target level
5. **Engagement options** — which ZenCloud engagement model fits the gap

---

## Example Recommendations by Gap

| Gap | Recommendation |
|-----|---------------|
| Level 1–2 in Decision Clarity | Decision Sprint — define and record the five most critical current decisions |
| Level 1–2 in Traceability | ADR implementation workshop — establish format, process, and first ten ADRs |
| Level 2–3 in Governance Speed | Architecture Governance Reset — lean governance model, board pack format |
| Level 3–4 in PMO Alignment | PMO Portal implementation — shared decision and status view |
| Level 3–4 in AI Readiness | AI Readiness Assessment — structured evaluation using the Velocity AI lens |
| Level 4–5 everywhere | Practitioner Certification — uplift internal team to Velocity-certified delivery |
