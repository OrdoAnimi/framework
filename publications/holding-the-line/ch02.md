# Chapter 2 — Intent Over Instruction

There are two ways to move a group of capable people toward an outcome. You can tell them exactly what to do, or you can tell them what you are trying to achieve and trust them to work out the how. The first feels safer. The second is faster, and it is the only one that scales.

The instinct under pressure is to instruct. Write the steps. Specify the sequence. Remove the ambiguity by removing the judgement. It feels like control, and for simple work it is enough. But the moment the work is complex, or the ground shifts after the order is given, detailed instruction becomes a liability. The people closest to the problem can see what you cannot, and you have told them not to act on it. You have built a machine that performs precisely the wrong thing with great fidelity, and then you stand over it, wondering why no one stopped.

Disciplined leadership inverts this. It gives intent — the outcome that matters, the constraints that are non-negotiable, the reason the outcome matters — and then extends the authority to meet that intent however the ground demands. Tell people what winning looks like and why, and you do not have to be in the room for them to make the right call. Tell them only what to do, and you have to be in every room, forever, and the work moves at the speed of your attention. Your attention does not scale. The intent does.

Intent is harder to write than instruction, which is why most leaders avoid it. Instruction hides behind volume. Intent cannot: it forces you to decide what actually matters and say so in a sentence a tired person can act on at midnight. *We will protect the customer’s data over the convenience of the release.* That is intent. *Follow the forty-step compliance checklist* is instruction, and the team that follows it to the letter can still ship the wrong thing — can satisfy every step and breach the purpose every step was meant to serve, and do so with a clear conscience, because they did what they were told.

Consider the leader who has just handed a difficult piece of work to a team. There are two versions of how the handover goes. In the first, the leader talks for forty minutes. They walk the plan step by step, anticipate every fork, prescribe the response to each, and leave behind a document so complete that judgement has been engineered out of it. The team is relieved. There is nothing to decide; there is only to comply. Then, a week in, reality does the one thing the document did not anticipate, and the team does the only thing the document trained them to do — it stops, and it escalates, and it waits, because the plan has no branch for this and no one has been given the right to invent one. The work halts at the precise moment it most needed someone to move.

In the second version, the leader speaks for ten minutes. They say what the outcome is and why it matters — what the organisation is actually trying to win, and what it will not trade away to win it. They name the two or three lines that cannot be crossed, and they are explicit that everything else is the team’s to decide. Then they say the sentence that makes the difference: *if the ground changes, I do not need you to ask me first; I need you to act on the intent and tell me what you did.* A week in, reality breaks the plan in the same place. This time the team does not stop. It recognises the situation as one the intent already covers, makes the call on the spot, keeps the outcome intact by changing the method, and tells the leader afterward what it changed and why. The work did not halt. It bent, and held.

The two handovers cost the leader the same forty minutes — ten of speaking and thirty of resisting the urge to keep prescribing. They produce opposite organisations. The first produces a team that can execute a plan and nothing else, that is brittle exactly where it needs to be supple, and that has been quietly taught that initiative is not wanted. The second produces a team that can carry the intent into conditions the leader never foresaw, which is the only thing that has ever actually scaled.

The discipline is this: say what the outcome is, name the lines that cannot be crossed, and then get out of the way. Intent over instruction is not a softer form of direction. It is a more demanding one. It asks the leader to be clear about the thing that is hardest to be clear about — what they actually want — and then to trust people with the rest. Instruction is what you reach for when you have not done the work of knowing your own intent, and would rather specify a hundred steps than commit to one sentence you can be held to.
