# Chapter 9 — Authorship and Ownership

There is a difference between authoring a decision and owning it, and the gap between the two is where most organisational dysfunction lives.

To author a decision is to write it down — to facilitate the discussion, frame the options, produce the document that records what was agreed. To own a decision is to be the named person who carries its consequence: who can be held to it when it proves costly, and who had the authority to make it in the first place. Healthy systems keep these together. Corrupted ones split them apart, and the split is almost always invisible until pressure arrives.

Watch what happens when it does. Picture the forum that signs off an important initiative. On paper everything is correct: an owner, a delivery lead, and the specialist closest to the work, all named, all responsible, all in the room. The document says *accountable*. Then a trade-off appears — the dates will not hold against the scope at the quality required — and the room performs a quiet, practised manoeuvre. The owner looks to delivery. Delivery looks to the specialist. The specialist names the constraint. And the constraint is received, acknowledged, and handed back down with a request to “find a way through.” No one pauses scope. No one says no to the senior stakeholder. No one re-baselines. Responsibility has been distributed around the table; authority has not.

This is the moment the gap becomes legible. **Ownership without authority is not ownership. It is exposure.** The named owner has been handed the consequence of a decision they were never empowered to make. Responsibility flows down; authority does not; and that gap is where velocity dies. The owner can be quoted against the outcome but could not have changed it — which is the worst position any leader can be placed in, because it manufactures blame while preventing the very act that would have averted it.

When the gap opens, the work does not stop. It continues by default, which is worse than stopping, because no one chose it and no one can be found to have chosen it. Trade-offs are deferred instead of resolved. Risk is pushed downstream toward whoever cannot refuse it. Escalation replaces judgement — every hard call climbs a ladder of approvals while the moment passes. And the people closest to the work learn the most corrosive lesson an organisation can teach: that surfacing a problem early changes nothing, so it is safer not to surface it at all. Symbolic ownership produces symbolic progress. The reports stay green; the work stays stuck.

Authorship is seductive precisely because it looks like leadership while carrying none of its cost. The author shapes the agreement, holds the pen, is visibly central to the decision — and walks away from its consequence the moment the consequence turns expensive. The system rewards this. It is comfortable, it is plausible, and it leaves no fingerprint. Ownership is the opposite. Ownership is findable on purpose. It is the leader who, when the trade-off appears, says *this is mine to call* and then makes the call, knowing the call attaches to their name for as long as the record exists.

The held line begins here, in this first refusal. The leader who holds it will not author a decision they do not own, and will not allow someone below them to be made the owner of a consequence they could not author. When the forum tries to hand the constraint back down, the leader sends it back up — to the authority that can actually resolve it — and stands there, in the discomfort of the unresolved room, until it is resolved by someone empowered to resolve it. That is not obstruction. It is the recognition that a decision and its consequence belong in the same pair of hands, and that an organisation which separates them has not collaborated. It has carefully arranged for no one to be responsible while everyone appears to be.

The corruption of leadership starts with this separation. Someone authors decisions they do not own. Someone owns consequences they did not author. The organisation calls the arrangement *shared accountability* and congratulates itself on its maturity. It is neither shared nor accountability. It is the disaggregation of a single load-bearing act into pieces too small for anyone to be caught holding — and the first thing the line has to refuse is the invitation to hold one of the pieces and call it ownership.

Keep the pair together. Author what you will own. Own what you author. Everything that follows in this movement is a variation on that one sentence, tested under heavier and heavier pressure, in rooms engineered to pull the two apart.
