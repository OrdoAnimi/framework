# Chapter 23 — The Eight Lenses

A decision that is clear to the person who made it can still die in a room, not because it is wrong but because it was never translated into the language of the people who have to own it. The titles change across a business, family, community, school, team, or place of worship, but the lenses remain: purpose, resources, risk, execution, people, systems, and stewardship.

Everyone in a leadership room is looking through a different lens, and each lens asks a different question. The chief executive asks about growth, timing, and strategic risk — *does this move us forward, and is now the moment?* The chief financial officer asks about capital, margin, and the confidence of the investment — *what does it cost, and how sure are we of the return?* The chief information officer asks about the platform, its resilience, and enterprise control — *does this strengthen the system or quietly fragment it?* The chief operating officer asks about operating rhythm and execution — *can we actually run this without breaking the line?* The marketing lead asks about the narrative and the demand — *what story does this tell the market, and does the market want it?* The risk and security leads ask about exposure and assurance and how much appetite there is for either — *what could go wrong, and who carries it?* And the chair asks the question that contains all the others — *is the decision clear, is the risk owned, and is the accountability explicit?*

The same proposal, rotated through these lenses, becomes seven different conversations — and the leader who can only speak one of those languages will lose the other six rooms. This is not spin. It is translation. The decision does not change; the framing does, so that each person who must commit to it can see it in the terms they are accountable for. Spin alters the decision to suit the audience. Translation holds the decision still and changes only the angle of light, so the same object is visible from every seat. The difference is not cosmetic. A leader who spins is eventually caught, because the seven versions do not reconcile. A leader who translates is trusted, because they always do.

Take one decision and turn it. A team wants to consolidate three ageing platforms onto one. To the chief information officer it is a resilience and control story — fewer points of failure, one place to govern. Rotate it to the chief financial officer and the same decision is a capital story — a write down now against a lower run cost later, and here is the payback period. Rotate it again to the chief operating officer and it is a cutover risk — the line cannot stop while you migrate, so when, and with what fallback. To the risk lead it is exposure during transition; to the chief executive it is whether the disruption buys enough future speed to be worth a hard year; to the chair it is whether someone owns the cutover by name and what happens if it slips. Six questions, one decision, and the recommendation only survives if it has already answered all six before it is presented from any one of them.

The eighth lens is the one easiest to forget and most costly to omit. Beyond the executives sit the chair, the board, the investor, the sponsor — the seats that do not run the organisation but answer for it. Their lens is duty, and duty asks the question the others assume someone else has handled: *is this decision clear, is the risk owned by a named person, and is the accountability explicit?* A recommendation can satisfy all seven operating lenses and still fail this one, because it left ownership vague. The eighth lens is where vagueness goes to die, and the leader who has not rotated through it arrives with a proposal that runs but does not stand.

This is a teachable skill, which is the point of naming it. The next person does not have to acquire the lenses by being humiliated in a boardroom and reconstructing afterward what they missed. They can learn to rotate a proposal through the lenses deliberately, in advance, until it holds from every angle — to take their own clean recommendation and ask, seat by seat, *what does this person see, and what will they object to.* The held line survives contact with the room when it has been translated for the room. The lenses are how.
