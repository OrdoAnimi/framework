# Chapter 7 — On the Record

A decision that cannot be quoted back was never really made.

The most corrosive habit in professional life is the unattributable answer — the call given verbally, in a corridor or a meeting that produces no record, so that it can be adjusted, denied, or reinterpreted later depending on how things turn out. It feels like flexibility. It is the opposite of leadership, because it places the decision and the accountability in different rooms. The person who gave the answer keeps the freedom to have meant something else; the person who acted on it keeps the exposure of having acted. The risk has been transferred while the authority has been retained, which is the precise signature of leadership refusing its own job.

Being on the record is the discipline of owning the call by name. The decision is stated plainly, attached to the person who made it, and held where the people affected can find it. This is uncomfortable for exactly the reason that makes it valuable: once a decision is on the record, it can be challenged, but only by engaging it; superseded, but only by a new decision owned by someone; never quietly disregarded, because the record shows it existed and who stood behind it. The record removes the most useful thing the avoidant leader has, which is deniability — the ability to have not-quite-said the thing they are now glad they can claim not to have meant.

There is a scene that shows the whole mechanism. A team needs a binding answer to proceed — an exception granted, a risk accepted, a constraint waived — and they go to the person with the authority to give it. They get a yes. But they get it in a meeting with no minute, or a quick word at a doorway, or a phone call, and they get it in language built for slipperiness: *yeah, I’m comfortable with that, run with it.* Comfortable. Run with it. Not *I approve this exception and I accept the risk; record me as the approver.* The team, relieved, proceeds. Six months later the risk materialises, the failure is reviewed, and the question is asked: who approved this? And the answer that comes back from the person who said *I’m comfortable, run with it* is some version of: *I don’t recall approving that. I may have said I understood their thinking. The decision was theirs to make.* The verbal yes has evaporated, exactly as it was designed to, and the team is holding a consequence for a decision that, on the record, was never made by anyone but them.

The disciplined version is unglamorous and takes thirty seconds. The same authority, asked the same question, says yes — and then writes, or asks someone to write, in a place that persists: *Approved by me on this date. Risk accepted on these terms. I am the owner of this exception.* That sentence does to the leader exactly what the verbal yes was designed to prevent. It fixes them. It makes them findable later, when being findable is uncomfortable. And that is the entire value of it, because a decision is only as real as the name that can be quoted against it.

There is a contrast here the book will return to. A boundary defended by a verbal reply is no boundary, because a verbal reply cannot be quoted, cannot be tested, and cannot protect anyone — it is structurally incapable of holding. It will be reinterpreted under pressure as whatever the pressure needs it to have meant. The record is what gives a boundary structural integrity: it converts a position into an artefact, and an artefact resists in a way that a remembered conversation never can. Standing on the record is what converts an opinion into a decision and a decision into something the organisation can build on. You can influence an outcome and still be bypassed; you cannot bypass a decision that is on the record without leaving a trace.

That trace is the whole point. The avoidant system runs on the absence of traces — on overrides that happened in no minute, exemptions granted by no named owner, directions given in language too soft to quote. It survives by ensuring that when things go wrong, there is no fixed point to push against, only a fog of plausible recollections in which everyone was, in their own account, reasonable. The leader who is willing to be quoted refuses to add to the fog. They give the organisation something it cannot get any other way: a fixed point. Everything else can move around it. But it has to be fixed, which means someone has to be willing to be named — and to be named in advance, before the outcome is known, while the naming still carries risk. Anyone will own a decision once it has proven right. The discipline is to own it while it could still go either way.
