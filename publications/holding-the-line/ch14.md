# Chapter 14 — Standing Is Not Influence

Standing is constantly confused with influence, and the confusion is expensive, because it leads leaders to invest in the wrong thing and then wonder why their boundaries do not hold.

They are not the same. Influence aligns. It shapes the discussion, builds consensus, moves people toward a view. It is real, and it is useful, and it is fragile under pressure, because influence persuades and persuasion can be overridden by the next, louder persuasion. Standing is different in kind. Standing determines whether a boundary holds when someone pushes against it. You can influence a decision and still be bypassed — the alignment you built simply dissolves the moment a senior stakeholder decides otherwise. **You cannot bypass standing without leaving a trace.** That is the whole distinction. Influence asks people to agree. Standing makes their disagreement visible, named, and owned. One depends on goodwill. The other does not.

Standing exists when a leader is embedded in the decision path itself — when the work cannot proceed without crossing the line they hold, when an override cannot happen without a named owner accepting it on the record, when a deviation cannot repeat without becoming visible at the level accountable for it. Watch the difference play out in the same escalation. A leader with influence sends a strong note expressing serious concern about a path; the path proceeds anyway, because nothing structural required it to stop, and the note becomes a footnote in a post-mortem nobody reads. A leader with standing holds a constraint the project cannot route around; to proceed against it, someone with a name must put that name to the override, in writing, where the organisation can see who chose what. The first leader recorded an opinion. The second forced a decision. The constraint did not change. The standing did.

This is why the test for standing is so unforgiving, and so simple. **If escalation is verbal, standing is implied** — which means it does not exist, because anything that lives only in conversation can be adjusted, denied, or quietly forgotten depending on how things turn out. If a deviation repeats without ever refining the boundary, standing is symbolic — the boundary is being crossed at will and the crossings are not accumulating into anything. If decisions expand in time as complexity rises, rather than collapsing into bounded calls, standing is weak — the leader is present but not load-bearing. None of these are reporting metrics. They are structural signals, and they tell you whether a leader’s position resolves anything or merely records that they were there.

Standing does not come from title. It does not come from owning a framework or a forum. It does not come from being liked, or senior, or right. It comes from being structurally unavoidable in the path of the decision — from the fact that the work has to come through you, and that when it tries to go around you, the going-around leaves a mark with a name on it. A leader can have enormous influence and no standing: consulted on everything, decisive on nothing, bypassed whenever the bypass is convenient. A leader can have modest influence and hard standing: not the most persuasive voice in the room, but the one whose boundary the room cannot cross without someone stepping forward to own the crossing.

For the leader holding the line, this is the difference between a position that records and a position that resolves. **Without standing, leadership records — with it, leadership resolves.** The leader who has only influence will spend their career producing well-reasoned views that the system absorbs and proceeds past, accumulating a private archive of having been right too quietly to matter. The leader who has built standing will spend it producing decisions — fewer of them, more expensive, more exposed, but binding. The work of the movement so far has been about what to refuse and when to refuse it. Standing is what makes the refusal hold. A boundary backed only by influence is a verbal reply with better manners. A boundary backed by standing is a line the organisation has to engage, by name, to cross — and that, not persuasion, is what lets a leader hold ground when the system is pushing to take it.
