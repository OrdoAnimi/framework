# Chapter 25 — The Rehearsal

Leadership is rarely difficult because the information is absent. It is difficult because time is short, interests conflict, and the outcome depends on how the decision is communicated under pressure — and pressure is the one condition you cannot study your way through. You can only rehearse it.

This is the part the profession leaves to chance. We prepare the analysis and assume the room will go as planned, and then the objection lands from an angle we did not expect, and the call that was sound on paper does not survive contact with a sceptical chair and a hostile clock. The leaders who hold their lines in those rooms are almost never improvising. They have been there before — if not in fact, then in rehearsal: they have framed the recommendation, taken the objection, defended the commercial value, absorbed the risk challenge, and refined the call, before the room was real.

The rehearsal has a shape, and the shape is the instrument itself, run as a loop. **Orient:** read the room you are about to enter — who is in it, which mindframe each one carries, where the line will be pushed. **Frame:** state the actual decision in plain language and rotate it through the lenses until it holds from every seat. **Pressure-test:** put the recommendation in front of its hardest objections and let them hit it — not the objections you wish you would get, but the ones the room will actually raise. **Refine:** find where it bent, fix that, and run it again. The loop is not a one-time preparation. It is a wheel you turn until the recommendation stops bending.

Set the rehearsal somewhere it can be seen. A delivery lead has a recommendation to pause a programme that is running on borrowed time. Alone, or with one trusted colleague playing the room, they run it. The colleague takes the commercial sponsor’s seat: *you’re telling me to stop the thing I promised the customer.* The lead answers, and hears their own answer wobble. They take the chief financial officer’s seat: *what does the pause cost versus the overrun you’re predicting?* The lead does not have the number, finds it, comes back. They take the chair’s seat: *who owns the decision to restart, and on what trigger?* By the fourth turn of the loop the recommendation has a number, a named owner, a restart trigger, and a single sentence that survives the sponsor’s first push. None of that was discovered in the real room. All of it was discovered here, where being wrong costs nothing.

Rehearsal is what turns the room-reading and the lenses into something that holds when the temperature rises. It is the boardroom run before the boardroom — the proposal pressure-tested against the objections it will actually face, the recommendation compressed until it can be stated and defended in the time the room will actually give it, the weak point found in practice rather than in the meeting where it costs you the decision. What you leave a rehearsal with is not a script. It is a recommendation you can defend against executive objection and a steadiness that does not come from hoping the room will be kind.

The reason to rehearse is the reason to do any of this: so the line holds when it is pushed. A line held by improvisation holds sometimes. A line held by someone who has already stood in the pressure and found where they bend holds far more often — and can be taught to the next person before they have to bleed for the lesson.
