# Chapter 3 — Intent That Travels

There is a single paragraph that lets capable people act correctly when you are not in the room. Learning to write it is the work.

The need for it appears anywhere plans meet reality. A manager hands over a shift. A parent leaves someone responsible for the household. A volunteer organiser cannot attend every event. A project lead delegates work across several teams. Detailed instructions can cover expected conditions, but they fail at the first condition no one predicted. What has to travel instead is purpose: what we are trying to achieve, why it matters, and which lines cannot be crossed.

Call this clear intent. It is a short, owned statement of *what we are trying to achieve and why* — pitched one level above the task, so that it survives the task changing. Not “build the integration by March.” That is instruction, and the moment March or the integration moves, it is useless. Rather: “we are trying to let the customer transact without re-entering their details, because the drop-off at that step is what is costing us the renewal — and we will not solve it by holding their data anywhere it isn’t protected.” Now the deadline can slip and the method can change and the constraint still holds and the purpose still steers. Someone three teams away, facing a fork you never imagined, can read that paragraph and know which way to turn.

A good intent paragraph does three things and resists a fourth. It states the purpose — the why behind the what, because the why is what survives when the what is overtaken by events. It states the desired end state — what the world looks like when this is done, concretely enough to recognise. And it states the boundaries — the few lines that may not be crossed in pursuit of it, the things you would rather fail than violate. What it resists is the temptation to keep going — to specify the steps, to colonise the how, to reach back into the gap it has just opened and fill it with instruction. Every step you prescribe is a piece of judgement you have taken back from the people who will need it.

But intent is only half the instrument. The other half is the part most leaders skip, and skipping it is why so much “empowerment” produces chaos. The other half is the playback: they tell *you* what they understood.

Picture the moment. A leader has given the intent — clearly, they believe — to the three people who will carry it. The instinct now is to ask “any questions?”, receive the polite silence that means nothing, and leave. The disciplined leader does the opposite. They say: *before we finish, tell me back what you’ve understood the intent to be, and what you’re going to do about it.* Then they go quiet and listen, because the next two minutes are the most valuable in the meeting. One of the three plays it back almost exactly. The second plays it back with a subtle distortion — they have heard the deadline as the purpose, and the purpose as a nice-to-have, which means under pressure they will protect the wrong thing. The third reveals, in their version, a constraint the leader never thought to state, because the leader knew it so well they forgot it was knowledge. None of this would have surfaced through “any questions?” All of it surfaces through the playback, because the playback tests the transmission, not the reception — it checks what actually arrived, not what was sent.

The leader corrects the second person’s distortion on the spot, while it costs a sentence rather than a quarter. They thank the third for the constraint and write it into the intent. And now the intent is no longer a thing the leader holds and hopes was understood. It is a thing three people can each state in their own words, which means it will survive the leader leaving the room — which was the entire purpose. An intent that lives only in the head of the person who issued it has not been transmitted. It has merely been spoken.

This is the difference between an instruction that breaks the moment conditions change and an intent that flexes to meet them. The instruction is the leader’s judgement, frozen at a point in time, distributed for compliance. The intent is the leader’s judgement, made portable, distributed for *enactment* — so that when the leader is not in the room, and they will not be in the room, the room still knows what they would want, and is trusted to do it. The single paragraph, well written and read back, is how one person’s clarity becomes many people’s authority to act. It is the most leverage a sentence can carry.
